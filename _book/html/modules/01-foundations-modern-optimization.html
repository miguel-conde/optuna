1  Foundations of Modern Optimization

This chapter establishes why modern optimization matters for Optuna users. Once objective evaluations become expensive, noisy, high-dimensional, or non-differentiable, the search strategy matters as much as the model itself.

By the end of the chapter, you should be able to explain what optimization problem Optuna is designed to solve, why naive search strategies break down, and why Bayesian optimization is a practical response.

1.1 Why This Matters

Suppose you are tuning a machine learning pipeline with a learning rate, tree depth, regularization strength, and feature preprocessing choices. Even before selecting a model, you already face a search problem over a mixed space of continuous, discrete, and categorical variables.

That is the setting where Optuna becomes useful: not ordinary calculus-based optimization, but efficient search over expensive black-box objectives.

1.2 Formal Definition of Optimization

In mathematical terms, optimization is the process of finding the best input parameters for a function.

Typically, we want to solve:

\[ x^* = \arg\min_{x \in \mathcal{X}} f(x) \]

where:

  • \(f(x)\) is the objective function.
  • \(x\) is the parameter vector.
  • \(\mathcal{X}\) is the search space.
  • \(x^*\) is the optimal parameter setting.

The problem can also be written as a maximization task:

\[ x^* = \arg\max_{x \in \mathcal{X}} f(x) \]

1.2.1 Example

Consider the problem of minimizing:

\[ f(x) = (x - 2)^2 \]

The optimal solution is:

\[ x^* = 2 \]

This is a simple convex function. In realistic problems, however, the objective may be non-convex, noisy, or expensive to evaluate.

In machine learning, for example, we often optimize an objective such as:

\[ f(x) = \text{validation error of a model} \]

where \(x\) represents hyperparameters.

1.3 Black-Box Optimization

Many real-world optimization problems are black-box functions. In these cases:

  • We cannot compute gradients.
  • We do not know the analytical form of the objective.
  • We can only evaluate the function at selected points.

Operationally, this means we can query \(f(x)\), observe the result, and use that observation to decide what to try next.

Examples include:

Problem Why it is black box
ML training Complex training dynamics
Simulations Physics or system engines
A/B experiments Real-world user behavior
Monte Carlo simulations Stochastic outputs

In these settings, traditional methods such as gradient descent, Newton methods, or closed-form optimization are often unavailable. Instead, we rely on sampling-based optimization.

1.4 Curse of Dimensionality

Search spaces grow exponentially with the number of parameters.

If each parameter has \(k\) possible values and we have \(d\) parameters, the total number of combinations is:

\[ k^d \]

For example:

Parameters Values each Total combinations
5 10 100,000
10 10 10,000,000,000

This makes exhaustive search infeasible very quickly. The consequence is not just that full search becomes slow, but that most of the space is never explored at all.

Important consequences:

  • Sampling efficiency becomes critical.
  • Intelligent search strategies matter more than brute force.
  • A poor search space definition can dominate the final result.

1.5 Grid Search vs Random Search

1.5.1 Grid Search

Grid search evaluates every combination in a predefined grid.

Example:

learning_rate depth
0.01 3
0.01 6
0.1 3
0.1 6

Advantages:

  • Simple.
  • Deterministic.
  • Easy to parallelize.

Disadvantages:

  • Scales exponentially.
  • Becomes inefficient in high dimensions.
  • Wastes evaluations on unimportant parameters.

1.5.2 Random Search

Random search samples parameters independently from the search space.

x1 = random()
x2 = random()
x3 = random()
...

The key practical insight is that random search often outperforms grid search when only a subset of parameters strongly affects performance. Grid search spends many evaluations covering dimensions that may not matter much, while random search covers more distinct combinations of the important ones.

1.6 Sample Efficiency

When evaluations are cheap, brute-force methods can be acceptable. But many real objectives are expensive:

Task Evaluation cost
ML training Minutes
Deep learning Hours
Simulation Minutes
Scientific experiment Days

If we can only afford a small budget, each evaluation should be as informative as possible. This is the idea of sample efficiency: finding strong solutions with as few objective evaluations as possible.

Random search does not use the results of earlier trials. Modern optimizers do.

1.7 Exploration vs Exploitation

Every optimizer must balance two competing goals.

1.7.1 Exploration

Explore unknown regions of the search space to discover potentially better areas.

1.7.2 Exploitation

Search near known good solutions to refine promising regions.

Too much exploration slows convergence. Too much exploitation can trap the search in a local optimum. Practical optimization algorithms succeed by balancing these two behaviors dynamically.

1.8 When Gradient-Based Methods Fail

Gradient-based methods work well when the objective is differentiable, gradients are available, and the optimization landscape is well behaved. Those assumptions fail in many real applications.

Problem Why gradients fail
Discrete parameters Gradients are undefined
Simulation-based models Gradients are unavailable
Non-differentiable functions Discontinuities
Noisy objectives Gradients become unstable

Examples of non-gradient-friendly choices include:

  • Number of trees.
  • Optimizer type.
  • Activation function.

These are categorical or discrete decisions, so gradient descent is not the right tool.

Common Mistake

Treating hyperparameter tuning as if the objective were smooth, convex, and differentiable leads to the wrong optimization strategy. In Optuna-style workloads, the objective is often mixed, noisy, and black box by construction.

1.9 Why Bayesian Optimization Works

Bayesian optimization is designed for expensive black-box functions. Instead of optimizing the true objective directly, it builds a surrogate model:

\[ \hat{f}(x) \]

The surrogate approximates the true objective using past observations. An acquisition function then decides which point should be evaluated next.

At a high level, the process is:

  1. Evaluate several candidate points.
  2. Fit a probabilistic model from the observed results.
  3. Estimate which regions look promising or uncertain.
  4. Evaluate the next candidate selected by the acquisition rule.
  5. Update the model.
  6. Repeat.

Advantages:

  • Uses information from previous trials.
  • Balances exploration and exploitation.
  • Reaches strong solutions in fewer evaluations.

That is why Bayesian optimization is widely used in hyperparameter tuning, robotics, engineering design, and reinforcement learning.

1.10 Where Optuna Fits

Optuna implements a modern Bayesian optimization workflow, with TPE (Tree-structured Parzen Estimator) as its default sampler.

Compared with Gaussian-process-based approaches, TPE is especially practical because it:

  • Scales better to higher dimensions.
  • Handles categorical variables naturally.
  • Works well with conditional search spaces.

We will study this algorithm in more detail in The TPE Algorithm.

1.11 Practical Exercise: Implement Random Search

As a first exercise, implement a simple optimizer for the function:

\[ f(x) = (x - 3)^2 + \sin(5x) \]

import math
import random


def objective(x):
    return (x - 3) ** 2 + math.sin(5 * x)


best_x = None
best_y = float("inf")

for _ in range(1000):
    x = random.uniform(-5, 5)
    y = objective(x)

    if y < best_y:
        best_y = y
        best_x = x

print(f"Best x: {best_x:.4f}")
print(f"Best value: {best_y:.4f}")
Best x: 3.4066
Best value: -0.8046

This optimizer does three things:

  1. Samples random points.
  2. Evaluates the objective.
  3. Keeps the best solution found so far.

Its limitations are exactly what motivate Optuna: it ignores previous evaluations and does not adapt to what it learns.

Try extending the exercise in three steps:

  1. Reduce the evaluation budget from 1000 to 50 and compare the result.
  2. Change the search interval and observe how sensitive random search is to space design.
  3. Rewrite the objective in two dimensions and see how quickly brute-force search degrades.

1.12 Recap

Modern optimization in machine learning and data science often involves:

  • Expensive black-box functions.
  • High-dimensional search spaces.
  • Discrete or non-differentiable parameters.

That combination makes classical exhaustive or gradient-based methods a poor default. Bayesian optimization improves sample efficiency by learning from prior evaluations, and Optuna is designed to make that workflow practical.

1.13 Decision Guide

Use the following rule of thumb:

  • Use grid search only for very small, fully discrete spaces.
  • Use random search as a strong baseline when evaluations are cheap.
  • Use Bayesian optimization when evaluations are expensive and each trial should teach the optimizer something.

Next, we will make those ideas precise in Bayesian Optimization Fundamentals.
